# Lec 4 Long-term Trade - in Finance market

![[../../attachments/Pasted image 20260505154733.png|667]]

In Japan, financial institutions are mostly banks, while in America there are banks and a lot of non-bank institutions. 

That's because Japan government supresses non-bank institutions by **lowering loan rate**. This lets companies to loan from banks more conveniently and caused Japan's loan-market-rely financial environment. Thus banks have more control over companies (compared to America).

*Note: When a non-bank institution established, its financial risk lies on itself; When a bank established, its financial risk is given to the whole society.*

**Cross-shareholding** (株式持ち合い かぶしきもちあい) is an important feature. It means that:
- main banks (メインバンク) and companies hold each other's share 
- companies hold other companies' share

In 1950s, Japanese broke tycoon (see lecture 2), to prevent hostile shareholding (opponent holding too much share), companies distributed share.

Advantage: lower trade cost, sharing risk
Disadvantage: due to interest, shareholders keep silent at shareholders' meeting

Q: What is the motivation of America's non-bank financial instutions' establishment?
A: To solve **information asymmetry**. This asymmetry causes adverse selection (sellers hide information to traders *before trading*) and moral hazard (guys don't follow the rules *after trading*). Non-bank institutions solves this problem by letting information becomes tradable. What's more, Japan and Germany solves this by long-term trading.

# Lec 7 Operation Strategy I - What is a Strategy

Michael Porter thinks Japanese companies have NO strategy. He listed these reasons:
- Japan's competitive edge comes from: raising productivity; lowering cost; enhancing management.
- But these 3 advantages can be imitated (for example, China).
- Production/Quality management is not strategy
=>Japanese companies have no strategy.

The nature of company strategy is the way to face comptition and get advantage in market. So we can use simple tables to create a strategy.
![[../../attachments/Pasted image 20260505201626.png|619]]


Porter mentioned an interesting example for the strategy: Suppose there are many different tribes in a grassland. They want to hunt, which strategy is better for each tribe?
- let wizard to decide where to hunt
- let the sage elder to decide where to hunt

The answer is to let wizard decide. Porter's explanation is: Each tribe's sage elder will give similar decisions because they will make decision with common knowledge and experience. If that, multiple tribes will swarm to one place and won't get any advantage. But if we let wizard to decide, they will give random decisions and divide the tribes in different places in the grassland.

# Lec 8 Operation Strategy II - Modular & Integrated (Important)

**One product** is composed of **different parts**, and it has **different usages** (yes, different usages, although we usually treat one product with only one usage).

If one part correspond to a single usage, then the product is **modularly assembled**. If one part correspond to multiple usages, then it is **integrally assembled**.

![[../../attachments/Pasted image 20260505210818.png|343]]

Modularly assembled products have one advantage: If we want to change a certain usage, we only need to adjust the part which correspond to that usage. 

However, for integrally assembled products, this is much harder. If we change one part of the product, more than one of the functionalities will change. This requires engineers to have the ability to finely adjust every parts of the product (which is **hard**), especially when it comes to product optimization.

For example, computer is a type of modularly assembled products. You can buy CPU, GPU, fan, hard disk separately and assemble them.

Jet F-35 is integrally assembled product. If an engineer wants to add/adjust a functionality, they has to consider whether changing this will affect the jet's radar-absorbent coating.

**Japanese companies handles integrated stuffs well**, but for modularly assembled products, they have had a hard time getting prepared for it. However, modern technologies and products are heading towards modularization, so Japan falls behind China, which is good at producing modulized stuffs at a low price and a fast pace.

After the change of how product assembles (integrated->modulized):

| Country | Gain                       | Loss                                             |
| ------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| China   | Employment opportunities   | Chances of innovation                            |
| America | High profit from designing | Employment opportunities                         |
| Japan   | NOTHING                    | Employment opportunities & Chances of innovation |

We have to mention that the integration method still have some advantages. For example, the MacOS system. Good integrally assembled products have better quality, because engineers have more chances to optimize on the integrated parts.
